INTRODUCTION

What does JENOBA do?

JENOBA distributes the data that turns a GNSS receiver into a centimeter-accurate positioning device. When a customer’s survey equipment, construction machinery, agricultural tractor, or drone receives signals from positioning satellites alone, the position is off by several meters because the satellite signals are distorted by the ionosphere, the troposphere, satellite-clock error, and ground-level multipath. JENOBA receives the live observations from the Geospatial Information Authority of Japan’s network of roughly 1,300 electronic reference stations (via the Japan Surveying Association), combines them through its own algorithm (Patent No. 5832050, which corrects for tectonic drift), and streams a virtual reference-station correction back to the customer. The customer’s position is then accurate to about two centimeters. The service is sold as a per-ID monthly subscription, embedded into the customer’s daily workflow ─ a subscription-software business on top of national-coordinate-grade infrastructure.

According to the annual securities report for FY2025 (year ended September 2025), revenue was ¥1,367M, of which 97% was data-distribution subscription and 3% was hardware resale. Operating margin was 56.6%, return on equity 15.8%, and the equity ratio 88.2%. Period-end subscriber count was 9,348 IDs, having grown at a 6.0% CAGR from FY21 to FY25 (FY21 subscribers were 7,393). The November 2025 FY25 briefing supplement explicitly states that “excluding fixed-term projects and proofs-of-concept, cancellation rates are extremely low.” FY2025 was the tenth consecutive year of record revenue.

What this report is about is the distance between “a very good business” and “a very good stock.” Business-level ROIC (operating income divided by invested capital) sits structurally above 20%, but headline ROE has slipped from 20.1% in FY21 to 15.8% in FY25, a 4.3-point drop (the path bottomed at 14.5% in FY24 and recovered to 15.8% in FY25 only because the buyback shrank the equity base). The reason is that cash is piling up faster than the company is returning it: in FY25 the firm executed a ¥740M buyback and paid out ¥79M in dividends, but operating cash flow was ¥589M and the cash balance still ended the year at ¥3.05bn (34% of market cap). The Q2 FY26 results released in May 2026 held the full-year guidance and reaffirmed a ¥1 dividend increase to ¥7, but the payout ratio stays at 17%. The remainder of this report walks through, in order, (1) how the stock has moved over the past two years, (2) the three questions the market is debating, (3) what could change over the next twelve months, and (4) what each scenario implies for the share price.

01 · PEAK REGIME After the April 2023 TSE Growth listing, the first full-year result (FY2023) showed revenue up 10% and subscriber additions of +626 IDs. The market took the recurring-subscription thesis at face value and bid the multiple up; by July 2024 the stock peaked at ¥875. The same period saw the broader market run hot on multi-GNSS expansion and the drone market, which lifted the multiple JENOBA was getting on the recurring base. Around that level the forward EV/EBIT was running near 15x and trailing P/E around 17x.

02 · CORRECTION Between late 2024 and spring 2025 the stock fell −38%, bottoming at ¥539 on 7 April 2025. Because the TOPIX total-return index was roughly flat over the same period (reference 1.05x), this was not just market beta. What changed was that period-end subscriber adds slowed (+535 IDs in FY24 narrowed to +284 in FY25) and ROE was visibly trending down from 20.1% in FY21. Real free float is also small once you strip out Topcon’s 10.57% strategic stake and the Minami family’s combined ~36%, so the liquidity premium also unwound.

03 · BUYBACK + RECORD EARNINGS In December 2024, alongside the FY24 results, management executed a large treasury-stock repurchase. In Q1 FY25 the company bought back ¥740M of stock (about 9% of the market cap at the time), which moved treasury shares from 607k to 1,607k and improved per-share metrics. Then the November 2025 FY25 results printed revenue of ¥1.37bn, an operating margin of 56.6%, and a tenth consecutive record year. On 28 November 2025 the company published an updated Business Plan and Growth Possibility document ─ which serves as the de facto medium-term plan for TSE Growth issuers. The stock recovered off the lows.

04 · CURRENT On 13 May 2026 JENOBA released its FY26 Q2 briefing supplement. The half-year showed revenue +4.8%, operating income +3.3%, and mid-period net income +4.2% ─ all record halves. Gross margin fell from 83.5% to 81.0% because the company refreshed its servers, booking ¥38M of tangible and ¥84M of intangible (software) capex in H1. Full-year guidance was held unchanged (revenue +4.8%, operating income +0.7%), and the dividend increase to ¥7 (from ¥6) was reaffirmed. At the current price of ¥669 the stock sits roughly mid-range of the 24-month band, with net cash equal to 34% of market cap. The remaining question is when, and how, that cash gets returned ─ through a payout ratio above 30%, another buyback, or M&A.

02 · CONTENTION

Three questions the market is debating

Margin durability, single-source upstream dependency, and the use of the cash pile ─ what the bulls and bears each see.

Contention 01 · Margin durability
Is the FY26 operating margin guide of 54.4% (down from 56.6%) a temporary server-refresh effect, or the start of a structural shift?
BULL
The margin compression can be explained by capex front-loaded into the first half. The Q2 tanshin and briefing supplement show H1 capex of ¥38M tangible plus ¥84M intangible — ¥122M, already above FY25’s full-year ¥91M. Meanwhile the underlying cost structure has not changed: electronic-reference-station data is bought from the Japan Surveying Association on a fixed annual contract, and each additional subscriber adds essentially no variable cost. Once the server refresh cycles through depreciation, the operating margin can return above 57%. The first observable check: whether H2 FY26 operating margin clears 55% and the full year beats the 54.4% guide.
BEAR
The bear read is that this is not just refresh capex ─ it is software investment that is starting to change the structure. Intangible assets went from ¥6M (FY24 end) to ¥61M (FY25 end) to ¥133M (Q2 FY26 end), a 22x increase in 18 months. On a five-year amortization assumption, the annual amortization charge will scale to ¥26–35M by FY27, on top of the existing ¥45M depreciation run-rate. If H1’s slower subscriber additions persist, the company faces “structurally higher cost, same growth rate” ─ which means margins settle around 50% rather than 56%. The bear case strengthens if the H2 disclosures do not name what the software capex was for or what return it is expected to generate.
Contention 02 · Upstream dependency
Is sourcing all reference-station data from a single counterparty a structural moat, or a single point of failure?
BULL
The Japan Surveying Association is the sole authorized distributor of GSI’s electronic reference-station data to commercial users. JENOBA has been a customer since the May 2002 public release ─ a 24-year relationship. The FY25 annual securities report describes the company’s mitigation approach: regular meetings with the Authority and the Association, redundant local data buffers, and active monitoring of station outages. New entrants would face the same single-counterparty bottleneck, which is what makes the moat real. On top of that, the Survey Act requires public-works surveying to use national-coordinate-aligned data, and JENOBA’s VRS method (Patent No. 5832050) is the only one in commercial use that delivers crustal-drift-corrected, national-coordinate output. The 24-year history of zero adverse price changes from the Japan Surveying Association is the basis for the bull view.
BEAR
JENOBA has no contractual leverage with the Japan Surveying Association. The YUHO risk section states explicitly that “the same data is provided only by the Japan Surveying Association; dependence is high.” The 24-year stable supply history does not guarantee that prices will stay flat ─ if anything, the longer the zero-revision streak runs, the larger the eventual adjustment risk. Separately, the QZSS CLAS / MADOCA broadcast services, which deliver correction data via the Japanese augmentation satellites themselves, are improving. If those services reach “good enough” accuracy for sub-meter use cases, JENOBA loses the entry of its price ladder (parts of surveying and agriculture). JII’s bridge: an upstream price revision flows directly into cost of sales, and the current 56% operating margin would not hold.
Contention 03 · Capital allocation
How does the ¥3.0bn net cash position (34% of market cap) get used ─ buyback, dividend, M&A, or another year of accumulation?
BULL
The ¥740M buyback in Q1 FY25 ─ about 9% of the market cap at the time ─ is evidence that management can deploy capital decisively when it wants to. The Business Plan document names M&A as “part of the strategy to expand the business,” alongside the April 2023 KDDI partnership for the drone vertical. With ¥3.0bn of net cash and zero debt, an acquisition in the ¥500M–¥1bn range is well within reach without any leverage; deploying it at the operating-business ROIC of around 23% would add ¥115–230M of annual operating income. If an M&A announcement or a second buyback materializes by FY27, the cash-discount component of the share price should compress.
BEAR
The bear read is that management talks about M&A but never executes. The Business Plan and Growth Possibility document has carried the line “M&A is also under consideration” for three years now, and three years after IPO no external transaction has happened. The payout ratio crept from 14.3% in FY24 to 14.7% in FY25 to 17.0% guided for FY26 ─ small steps relative to the cash generation. Investment securities yield about 1.2%, well below the operating-business returns. JII’s bridge: every ¥1bn of idle cash earns roughly ¥10–15M of investment income, versus around ¥230M if it were deployed at the operating ROIC. That arithmetic explains most of the ROE slide from 20.1% in FY21 to 15.8% in FY25; if the cash continues to sit idle, ROE keeps drifting down structurally.
